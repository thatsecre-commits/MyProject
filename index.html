Overview

The First Nations peoples lived across Canada. Many were not nomadic, meaning they stayed in one place where resources supported them. The six broad groups are the Woodland, Iroquois, Plateau, Plains, Pacific Coast, and Inuit nations. Each group adapted to its surroundings and climate.

Woodland First Nations

The Woodland nations lived among dense forests and valued birch bark for making canoes, tools, and utensils. With plentiful wood and natural resources, they were mostly settled and not nomadic.

Iroquois First Nations

The Iroquois built well-planned villages near fresh water, farmland, and forests ‚Äî a combination that supported permanent communities. Their climate was moderate, making farming and settlement easier.

Plateau First Nations

The Plateau peoples had many sweet plants and tree sap resources. Berries and forests provided food and building materials, allowing them to stay settled. Rapid waterways meant most travel was done on foot.

Plains First Nations

The Plains nations lived in open grasslands with few trees, making wood a rare and valuable resource. Many were nomadic, following herds and seasonal patterns to find food and shelter.

Pacific Coast First Nations

The Pacific Coast nations lived in regions with mild, stable climates and lush forests. Abundant plants and marine life supported large, permanent villages rich in culture and art.

Inuit

The Inuit lived in Arctic regions with extreme cold and little to no trees. They valued wood and adapted to long daylight hours in summer and short days in winter, developing unique tools and lifestyles for survival in harsh conditions.

Conclusion

Each First Nation adapted to its environment ‚Äî forests supported permanent villages, while open or frozen areas encouraged mobility. Their resourcefulness and connection to the land remain an important part of Canada's history and culture today.
